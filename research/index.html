My research centers on foundational questions concerning human reasoning ability, intelligence, and cognitive skill acquisition. In particular, I am currently studying how adults and children learn abstract reasoning skills, and how these abilities are shaped by sociocultural mechanisms such as formal education.

As a point of departure, I assume that the so-called Flynn effect—the striking tendency for IQ scores to rise over time—largely reflects changes in cognitive skill and dispositional thinking styles brought on by the advent of modern industrialized society. These changes are characterized, among other things, by improved capacities for abstract reasoning, rule induction, and categorization—abilities that psychologists often group under the label fluid intelligence.

In recent decades, the weight of scientific evidence is increasingly favoring the hypothesis that formal education is a significant driver of the Flynn effect. Although this is an important step for understanding the proximal causes of human intelligence, we still lack an understanding of the Flynn effect at the level of cognition: Given exposure to a proximal cause such as formal education, what changes occur in the mind of the individual that translate into improved capacities for abstract reasoning? My research aims to take a small step in filling this gap in our understanding.

In order to study these questions, I employ behavioral experiments, neuroimaging, and computational cognitive modeling.
